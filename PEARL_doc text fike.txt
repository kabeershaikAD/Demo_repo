1.INTRODUCTION
1.1 BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
The contemporary landscape of autonomous Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems faces a significant "Orchestration Crisis." While agentic architectures—systems that autonomously plan and execute complex workflows—promise to revolutionize domains like legal research, healthcare, and education, their practical deployment is hindered by severe infrastructural bottlenecks. Current state-of-the-art orchestration frameworks rely heavily on monolithic, cloud-based Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 to function as the central reasoning engine or "brain." This dependency introduces the "Cloud Trap," characterized by three persistent barriers  prohibitive recurring costs due to token-based billing, high latency that renders real-time interaction impractical, and critical privacy vulnerabilities arising from the transmission of sensitive data to third-party servers.
This challenge is particularly pronounced in sensitive sectors such as the judiciary and defence, where Data Sovereignty regulations (e.g., GDPR, local data laws) strictly prohibit cloud-based processing of confidential information. Existing alternatives fail to bridge this gap rule-based orchestrators are computationally efficient but lack the adaptability to handle ambiguous queries, while monolithic LLM orchestrators offer high reasoning capabilities but are economically and operationally unsustainable for widespread adoption.
The fundamental problem addressed in this research is the development of a performance-efficient, automated system capable of intelligently coordinating diverse AI agents entirely offline. Recent advances in Knowledge Distillation have shown promise in compressing semantic understanding, yet its application to Orchestration Logic—the sequential decision-making required to manage workflows—remains an unexplored frontier. There is a critical need for a framework that decouples sophisticated reasoning from massive model size, enabling advanced agentic capabilities on resource-constrained hardware.



1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT 
The deployment of Multi-Agent Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems in privacy-sensitive and resource-constrained environments is fundamentally hindered by three pervasive limitations high operational costs, significant latency bottlenecks, and critical data sovereignty risks. Existing agentic frameworks predominantly rely on token-based commercial APIs for sophisticated orchestration logic—such as routing, planning, and verification—rendering them economically unsustainable for high-volume public use. Concurrently, the round-trip time required for cloud-based reasoning introduces substantial latency, often ranging from 2 to 5 seconds per step, which makes real-time, interactive user experiences impractical. Furthermore, the necessity of transmitting sensitive user queries and retrieved document contexts to third-party cloud servers directly violates strict regulatory mandates like GDPR and local data laws, particularly in sectors such as law and defence. Consequently, there is a critical absence of lightweight, offline-capable orchestration frameworks that can match the decision-making fidelity of large models while operating efficiently on standard consumer hardware
1.3 OBJECTIVES  
The principal aim of this research is to develop a Performance-Efficient Agentic?RAG?Framework (PEARL) that achieves near-expert orchestration accuracy using compact, locally deployable models. To achieve this overarching aim, the work is structured around the following specific objectives  
* Design of a Lightweight Orchestration Framework 
To construct a modular multi-agent architecture that coordinates domain-specific retrieval, reasoning, and verification tasks through a distilled Small Language Model (SLM), eliminating the need for cloud-based controllers.
* Development of an Evaluation?and?Benchmarking?Suite
To introduce standardized quantitative metrics—specifically Routing Accuracy Score (RAS) and Workflow Appropriateness Index (WAI)—for rigorously measuring orchestration quality, latency, and computational efficiency.
* Deployment?in?a?Privacy-Preserving?Environment 
To enable fully offline or air-gapped deployment suitable for institutional and legal-domain applications, ensuring that no sensitive data leaves the local infrastructure.
1.4 SCOPE OF THE PROJECT  
 The 	scope 	of 	this 	project 	encompasses 	the 	design 	and 	development 	of PEARL (Performance-Efficient Agentic RAG through Learned Orchestration), a lightweight and modular orchestration framework that enables efficient coordination using small language models?(SLMs). It is structured around a JSON-based messaging protocol and reproducible workflow traces to achieve high-quality orchestration with minimal computational overhead. The framework integrates five key components  a modular orchestration architecture with well-defined APIs and structured trace logging; an expert-driven trace collection pipeline in which large teacher models?(GPT-3.5/4)?generate optimal routing and sequencing decisions; a knowledge-distillation process for training Flan-T5-small/base models to replicate expert orchestration using workflow-coherence objectives and invalid-sequence penalties; workflow-optimization techniques such as dependency pruning, complexity-aware routing, and latency profiling; and a comprehensive evaluation suite employing the Routing Accuracy Score?(RAS), Workflow Appropriateness Index?(WAI), and RAGAS?metrics to benchmark orchestration quality. The project focuses on transferring orchestration intelligence into compact SLMs rather than creating new retrieval or generation models, covering system design, orchestration data acquisition, fine-tuning pipelines, workflow-graph optimization, and end-to-end benchmarking, while excluding production-scale infrastructure (Kubernetes?deployment, authentication?layers, and cross-domain?expansion), which are reserved for future work.

Large Language Models (LLMs) have evolved from generic text generation systems into autonomous reasoning engines capable of retrieval, planning, and feedback-driven decision-making. This transition from static prompt-response architectures to interactive computational systems has redefined the scope of Artificial Intelligence (AI). Despite these advancements, the operational deployment of such systems remains restricted by high resource requirements and data-governance barriers. Current state-of-the-art orchestration frameworks depend on commercial cloud models for coordination and workflow routing, which introduces elevated runtime costs, latency that limits real-time performance, and impossibility of compliant deployment in privacy-sensitive domains .
 2.1.1 DEFINITIONS AND KEY CONCEPTS
* Orchestration in RAG  
The process of coordinating various specialized agents (retrievers, generators, verifiers) to execute a complex query. In PEARL, this involves determining the optimal sequence of agent invocations.
* Knowledge Distillation 
 A compression technique where a small "student" model (e.g., Flan-T5) is trained to mimic the decision-making patterns of a large "teacher" model (e.g., GPT-4), transferring intelligence while reducing computational load [1].
* Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
  A framework that retrieves external knowledge to ground LLM responses, reducing hallucinations and improving factual accuracy [6].
* Small Language Models (SLMs) 
 Compact language models (typically under 1B parameters) designed for efficient inference on local hardware, offering a balance between performance and resource usage.
To fully appreciate the architectural innovations of PEARL, it is necessary to define the core technological paradigms that underpin the system. This section details the specific definitions of RAG, Orchestration, Distillation, and Data Sovereignty as they apply to this project.
2.1.2 RETRIEVAL-AUGMENTED GENERATION (RAG) AND AGENTIC WORKFLOWS
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a hybrid AI architecture that enhances the accuracy and reliability of Large Language Models (LLMs) by grounding their responses in external, verifiable data. Unlike standard LLMs, which rely solely on "parametric memory" (knowledge stored in weights during training), RAG systems utilize "non-parametric memory" (vector databases containing up-to-date documents). When a query is received, the system retrieves relevant chunks of information and injects them into the LLM's context window.
However, PEARL moves beyond Standard RAG—which typically follows a fixed, linear path (Retrieve -->   Generate) to Agentic RAG. In an Agentic RAG system, the workflow is not hard-coded. Instead, the system treats retrieval, generation, and verification as distinct "tools" or "agents." The system dynamically decides 
1. Whether retrieval is necessary (e.g., skipping retrieval for greeting queries).
2. The depth of retrieval (e.g., fetching 3 documents for a fact check vs. 20 documents for a legal summary).
3. Whether the generated answer requires secondary verification.
This distinction is critical because Agentic RAG allows for higher efficiency and accuracy but requires a sophisticated "brain" or controller to make these routing decisions in real-time.
2.1.3 MULTI-AGENT ORCHESTRATION AND WORKFLOW ROUTING
Multi-Agent Orchestration refers to the computational logic that governs the interaction between specialized AI agents. In a modular AI system, different models or functions act as agents—one might be an expert in keyword search (Retriever), another in drafting text (Generator), and another in checking facts (Verifier). Orchestration is the process of coordinating these agents to solve a complex user query.
In current state-of-the-art frameworks (like LangChain or MetaGPT), orchestration is typically handled by a "Router" or "Controller." This controller interprets the user's intent and constructing a Workflow Graph—a sequence of steps required to fulfil the request. For example, for a legal query, the graph might be  [Query Booster --> Legal Retriever --> Citation Verifier --> Answer Generator].
The core problem PEARL addresses is that this orchestration layer usually depends on massive, general-purpose LLMs (like GPT-4). This creates a "Cloud Trap," where the logic to route a simple task costs as much computational power as the task itself. PEARL redefines orchestration as a learnable prediction task, where a small model learns to predict the optimal workflow graph based on the query features.
2.1.4 KNOWLEDGE DISTILLATION AND ORCHESTRATION TRANSFER
Knowledge Distillation (KD) is a model compression technique where a compact "student" model is trained to reproduce the behaviour of a large, complex "teacher" model. Historically, KD has been used to transfer semantic understanding (e.g., teaching a small model to classify sentiment like a large model).
PEARL introduces a novel application of this concept Orchestration Distillation. Instead of teaching the student model to generate the answer (which requires vast world knowledge), PEARL teaches the student to generate the plan. The student model (Flan-T5-small) observes thousands of "Expert Traces"—logs of how GPT-4 routed diverse queries—and learns the underlying logic of efficient coordination. This involves transferring the "Dark Knowledge" of decision-making learning not just which agent to call, but the confidence thresholds and fallback logic used by the expert teacher.
2.1.6 SMALL LANGUAGE MODELS (SLMS) AND EDGE QUANTIZATION
Small Language Models (SLMs) are a class of neural networks, typically under 1 billion parameters, designed for efficiency rather than encyclopedic knowledge. Examples include Microsoft's Phi-3, Google's Gemma, and the Flan-T5 family used in this project. SLMs are critical for "Edge AI"—running intelligence on local devices (like office servers or laptops) rather than in centralized data centers.
To enable these models to run on non-specialized hardware, PEARL employs Quantization. This is the process of reducing the precision of the model's internal weights, for example, converting 32-bit floating-point numbers to 8-bit or 4-bit integers. Techniques like QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation) allow these compressed models to be fine-tuned on specific tasks without losing their reasoning capabilities. In PEARL, quantization is the enabler that will allow the Orchestrator to run with <150ms latency on a standard CPU, ensuring the overhead of coordination does not slow down the user experience.
2.1.5 DATA SOVEREIGNTY AND COMPLIANCE-BY-DESIGN
Data Sovereignty refers to the concept that data generated by an individual or organization is subject to the laws and governance structures of the nation or industry where it resides. In the context of AI, this translates to the requirement that sensitive data (e.g., legal case files, student records) must not leave the institution's secure perimeter.
Air-Gapped Architecture is a security measure where the computer network is physically isolated from unsecured networks, such as the public internet. For AI systems, this means no calls to external APIs (like OpenAI or Anthropic). PEARL adopts a Compliance-by-Design approach, meaning the system is architected from the ground up to function without internet access. By relying on locally hosted SLMs and vector databases, PEARL ensures that no data ever crosses the organizational boundary, making it compliant with strict regulations like GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) and professional codes of conduct (e.g., Attorney-Client Privilege)
2.2 TAXONOMY OF RELATED TECHNOLOGIES
To understand the positioning of PEARL, it is essential to classify the vast body of literature into distinct technological paradigms. The research landscape for efficient, private, and agentic AI does not exist in a single domain but sits at the intersection of model compression, information retrieval, and autonomous control systems.
CATEGORY 1
Model Compression and Distillation 
The first major branch of the taxonomy concerns Model Compression, the science of reducing the computational footprint of neural networks while retaining their capabilities. This domain is critical for enabling "Edge AI"—the ability to run sophisticated models on local hardware (like standard servers or Raspberry Pis) rather than cloud clusters.
Teacher-Student Distillation  This is the foundational paradigm where a large, complex "teacher" model trains a smaller "student" model. The taxonomy here splits into Response-Based Distillation (mimicking the final output), Feature-Based Distillation (mimicking internal activation maps), and Relation-Based Distillation (mimicking the relationships between data examples). Early works focused on classification tasks, but the field has evolved toward Reasoning Distillation, where the student learns the process of deriving an answer (Chain-of-Thought) rather than just the answer itself.
Quantization Strategies
 This involves reducing the precision of the model's weights (e.g., from 32-bit floating point to 4-bit integers). The taxonomy distinguishes between Post-Training Quantization (PTQ), applied after training, and Quantization-Aware Training (QAT), applied during training. Recent breakthroughs like QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation) have created a new sub-category of "Efficient Fine-Tuning," allowing massive models to be adapted on consumer hardware without losing the benefits of their pre-training.
CATEGORY 2  
Retrieval-Augmented Architectures 
 The second branch covers RAG Architectures, which define how external knowledge is integrated into generation. The evolution of this field moves from static to dynamic systems.
Static RAG  
In this classical approach, the retrieval step is fixed. For every user query, the system retrieves a pre-determined number of documents (top-$k$) and feeds them to the generator. This architecture is robust but inefficient; it wastes resources on simple queries and fails on complex ones requiring deep research.
Adaptive and Dynamic RAG  
This emerging paradigm introduces decision-making into the retrieval loop. Systems in this category assess the query's complexity or the model's uncertainty before retrieving. The taxonomy further divides into Iterative Retrieval (multi-step search), Recursive Retrieval (query decomposition), and Self-Reflective RAG (where the model critiques its own retrieval quality).
Hybrid Retrieval Systems  
These systems combine dense retrieval (vector embeddings) with sparse retrieval (BM25 keyword matching) and symbolic filtering (metadata tags). This is particularly relevant for domain-specific applications like law or medicine where exact keyword matches (e.g., case IDs) are as important as semantic meaning.
CATEGORY 3
 Orchestration and Multi-Agent Control 
The third and most complex branch is Orchestration Control. This defines the "brain" of a multi-agent system—the logic that dictates which agent acts when.
Rule-Based Orchestration (SOPs) 
These systems rely on hard-coded logic trees or Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). For example, "If the query contains the word 'code', call the Coding Agent." While fast and deterministic, these systems are brittle and cannot handle ambiguity or novel tasks.
Cloud-Centric LLM Orchestration  
This is the current state-of-the-art (e.g., LangChain, AutoGen). A massive general-purpose LLM (like GPT-4) acts as the router. It parses the state and decides the next step. While highly capable, this approach suffers from the "Cloud Trap"—high latency, high cost, and privacy exposure.
Graph-Based and Evolutionary Routing
 A theoretical niche where the workflow is modelled as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) that can be optimized using evolutionary algorithms or reinforcement learning. These systems attempt to find the "optimal path" through a network of agents but often require immense computational resources to converge.
CATEGORY 4 
 Privacy and Governance
 The final branch addresses the non-functional requirements of AI deployment, specifically in regulated industries.
Offline Architectures 
 These designs prioritize "Data Sovereignty." They require that all inference, storage, and processing occur within a secured perimeter with no internet access. This necessitates the use of SLMs and quantized models discussed in the first branch.
* Compliance-Driven Architectures 
 These systems embed legal or ethical rules directly into the workflow. For example, "Access Control RAG" filters retrieved documents based on user clearance levels before the LLM sees them.
* Domain-Adaptive Models 
 This category includes models pre-trained or fine-tuned on specific corpora (e.g., LegalBERT, BioGPT) to handle the jargon and reasoning patterns of a vertical industry, often outperforming much larger generalist models in their niche.
2.3 DETAILED REVIEW OF EACH CATEGORY
2.3.1 COMPRESSION AND KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER
The pursuit of efficient AI began with the realization that massive neural networks contain significant redundancy. Hinton et al. (2015) pioneered the concept of Knowledge Distillation (KD), demonstrating that a small "student" network could achieve high performance by learning to mimic the "soft targets" (probability distributions) of a larger "teacher" network, rather than just the hard class labels. They introduced the concept of "dark knowledge"—the information hidden in the relationships between incorrect classes—which proved vital for training robust small models. PEARL extends this foundational concept by applying it to a new domain  orchestration traces. instead of distilling class probabilities, PEARL distills the decision logic of a workflow.
Following this, Sanh et al. (2019) introduced DistilBERT, which applied KD to the Transformer architecture. They successfully reduced the size of BERT by 40% while retaining 97% of its performance on the GLUE benchmark. Their methodology of initializing the student with a subset of the teacher's layers and using a triple-loss function (cosine, masked language modeling, and distillation loss) established the standard for NLP compression. Jiao et al. (2020) refined this with TinyBERT, introducing layer-wise distillation that transfers knowledge not just from the final output, but from the intermediate attention matrices and hidden states. This allowed for even greater compression ratios (4x smaller), proving that "deep" knowledge transfer is more effective than surface-level imitation.
The field took a leap forward with Mukherjee et al. (2023) and their Orca model. Unlike previous works that focused on classification or simple generation, Orca focused on distilling reasoning capabilities. They trained a 13B parameter model on "explanation traces"—step-by-step reasoning chains generated by GPT-4. This proved that a smaller model could learn how to think if provided with enough examples of the teacher's thought process. PEARL directly adopts this "explanation tuning" philosophy but adapts it for agentic routing rather than general reasoning.
Finally, Dettmers et al. (2023) revolutionized the hardware accessibility of these techniques with QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation). By introducing 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantization and double quantization, they made it possible to fine-tune massive models on consumer-grade GPUs (like a single RTX 4090) without performance degradation. This technology is the enabler for PEARL's deployment strategy, allowing the system to run on commodity hardware found in public institutions
 2.3.2  RETRIEVAL-AUGMENTED SYSTEMS AND RETRIEVAL
OPTIMIZATION
The integration of external knowledge into LLMs was formalized by Lewis et al. (2020) with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Their work demonstrated that combining a dense retriever (DPR) with a sequence-to-sequence generator (BART) allowed models to tackle knowledge-intensive tasks without needing to memorize the entire internet. However, the original RAG implementation was static—it always retrieved k documents, creating a bottleneck for simple queries (latency) and complex queries (insufficient context).
Addressing this rigidity, Asai et al. (2023) proposed Self-RAG, a framework where the LLM learns to critique its own retrieval. By training the model to generate special reflection tokens (e.g., [Retrieve], [IsRelevant]), the system could dynamically decide when to use external data. While effective at reducing hallucinations, Self-RAG introduced significant inference overhead due to the constant generation of control tokens. Niklaus et al. (2024) in AutoRAG systematically studied the trade-offs of retrieval parameters, finding that the optimal retrieval depth (k) varies significantly by query type.
In the engineering domain, Abbineni et al. (2025) developed MuaLLM, a multi-stage hybrid RAG system for circuit design. They introduced a pipeline of "Coarse Retrieval --> Fine Retrieval --> Re-ranking --> Verification." This explicit staging improved accuracy for technical queries but relied on a fixed, linear workflow. Elango et al. (2020) further validated the importance of Document Re-Ranking, showing that adding a cross-encoder step after retrieval could improve answer F1 scores by 6-8%, albeit at the cost of higher latency.
PEARL synthesizes these findings by creating a Learned Orchestrator that dynamically composes these stages. Instead of a fixed pipeline (Mua LLM) or expensive self-reflection tokens (Self-RAG), PEARL's controller predicts the optimal configuration (e.g., "Skip re-ranking for this simple query") in a single forward pass, optimizing the latency-accuracy trade-off discussed in AutoRAG.
2.3.3 ORCHESTRATION CONTROLLERS
The coordination of multiple AI agents has become a central challenge in 2024-2025. Hong et al. (2024) introduced MetaGPT, which famously encoded "Standard Operating Procedures" (SOPs) into agent roles. By defining rigid personas (Product Manager, Architect, Engineer) and communication protocols, they achieved state-of-the-art results in code generation. However, MetaGPT relies on rule-based logic for state transitions, limiting its flexibility in unstructured domains like legal research.
Zhang et al. (2024) proposed AgentNet, which models agent interaction as a dynamic graph optimization problem. Using evolutionary algorithms, AgentNet can "evolve" the routing structure to fit the task. While highly adaptive, the computational cost of evolving a graph in real-time is prohibitive for latency-sensitive applications. Wang et al. (2025) introduced Confucius, an intent-driven orchestration framework for network management. Confucius demonstrated that a hierarchical approach—separating high-level intent from low-level execution—improves system reliability.
A critical limitation across all these frameworks is the reliance on Cloud LLMs. Rasal and Hauer (2024) evaluated multi-agent systems for abstract reasoning and noted that the "Meta-Controller" (the brain routing the tasks) is typically GPT-4. This creates a single point of failure and massive cost. Li et al. (2023) explored Adaptive LLM Routing using Reinforcement Learning to select tools, but their approach was limited to single-step tool selection, not multi-step workflow sequencing.
PEARL advances this field by replacing the Cloud LLM and the Rule-Based SOPs with a Distilled Policy Network. By treating orchestration as a sequence generation task (Query -> Workflow Token Sequence), PEARL achieves the adaptability of AgentNet and the structure of MetaGPT, but runs locally with the efficiency of a small model.
2.3.4 PRIVACY, ETHICS, AND LEGAL AI
The deployment of AI in sensitive sectors is constrained by governance requirements. O'Neill et al. (2024) analyzed "Trustworthy AI in Institutional Settings," identifying Data Sovereignty as the primary barrier to adoption. They argued that institutions cannot use systems that "leak" data to model providers. Lin et al. (2024) surveyed privacy-preserving architectures and concluded that Air-Gapped Local Inference is the only robust solution for enterprises, as techniques like masking or differential privacy often degrade performance.
In the legal domain, Paul et al. (2022) developed InLegalBERT, a model pre-trained on Indian legal documents. They demonstrated that domain-specific pre-training significantly outperforms generalist models (like BERT) on tasks like case outcome prediction and statute identification. Malik et al. (2021) released the ILDC (Indian Legal Documents Corpus), providing a benchmark for legal reasoning.
However, a major gap exists in Compliance-Driven Orchestration. Rao et al. (2025) proposed policy-based access control for RAG, filtering documents based on user roles. While effective for retrieval, this doesn't address the broader orchestration lifecycle. OpenPrivacy Lab (2023) issued guidelines for ethical model hosting, explicitly recommending "Local Inference" and "Audit Logging."
PEARL is designed as a direct response to these findings. By utilizing InLegalBERT for its retrieval embeddings and training on the ILDC corpus, it ensures domain relevance. More importantly, its fully offline architecture (enabled by the distillation techniques reviewed in Section 2.2.1) satisfies the strict air-gapping requirements laid out by Lin et al., providing a compliant solution that existing cloud-based orchestrators cannot offer.

2.5 TREND ANALYSIS
Current advancements in Artificial Intelligence and Multi-Agent Systems indicate three decisive trends that are reshaping the landscape of technological development. These trends collectively signal a move away from centralized, monolithic models toward decentralized, efficient, and rigorously benchmarked architectures. This section details these shifts and their relevance to PEARL.
2.5.1 THE PARADIGM SHIFT  FROM CLOUD-CENTRIC TO EDGE-NATIVE AI
A foundational shift is occurring in AI deployment strategies, moving from "Cloud-First" to "Edge-Native" architectures. Historically, the immense computational requirements of Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4 necessitated centralized hosting in massive GPU clusters. This created a dependency model where institutions merely accessed intelligence via APIs, surrendering control over latency, cost, and data privacy.
However, recent geopolitical and regulatory pressures (such as the EU AI Act and GDPR) are forcing a re-evaluation of this model. Lin et al. (2024) and the OpenPrivacy Lab (2023) document a growing demand for "Data Sovereignty," where sensitive data never leaves the user's premises. Concurrently, the rise of high-performance Small Language Models (SLMs) like Microsoft's Phi-3 and Google's Gemma has proven that high reasoning capabilities can be achieved with <5 billion parameters.
* Relevance to PEARL 
 PEARL embodies this trend by proving that Orchestration—the most complex part of an agentic system—does not require a 175B parameter model. By distilling this capability into an 80M parameter Flan-T5 model, PEARL enables sophisticated multi-agent coordination on standard edge hardware (like local servers or high-end laptops), effectively decoupling intelligence from cloud dependency.
2.5.2 EVOLUTION OF ORCHESTRATION  FROM STATIC RULES TO LEARNED POLICIES
The mechanisms for controlling multi-agent systems are undergoing a rapid evolution. The first generation of agents (e.g., AutoGPT) relied on fragile loops. The second generation (e.g., MetaGPT) introduced "Standard Operating Procedures" (SOPs)—hard-coded roles and rules (e.g., "Always have the Code Reviewer check the Developer's code"). While stable, these rule-based systems are rigid and cannot adapt to novel queries.
The current state-of-the-art (e.g., AgentNet, LangGraph) utilizes large LLMs as dynamic routers, allowing for flexible, context-aware decision-making. However, this incurs the "Cloud Trap" of high cost and latency. The emerging trend, validated by Wang et al. (2025) with Confucius, is Policy Learning—training dedicated, lightweight neural networks to handle routing decisions.
* Relevance to PEARL 
 PEARL pioneers the application of Orchestration Distillation. Instead of relying on static SOPs (which are too rigid) or Cloud LLMs (which are too expensive), PEARL treats orchestration as a learnable prediction task. This represents the next logical step in the evolution of control systems  a learned, frozen policy that offers the flexibility of an LLM with the speed of a rule-based system.
2.5.3 DEMOCRATIZATION OF FINE-TUNING VIA PARAMETER-EFFICIENT METHODS
Finally, there is a trend toward the Democratization of Model Adaptation. Historically, fine-tuning an LLM required industrial-scale hardware. The introduction of QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation) by Dettmers et al. (2023) changed this landscape overnight. It enabled the fine-tuning of massive models on consumer-grade GPUs by keeping the backbone frozen in 4-bit precision and only training a small set of adapters.
This has led to an explosion of "Domain-Specific Models" (e.g., Medical-Llama, Law-GPT), where small teams can adapt state-of-the-art models to their specific niche without needing a data center.
* Relevance to PEARL 
 PEARL leverages QLoRA to fine-tune its generator agents. More importantly, it demonstrates that Orchestration Capability itself is a domain that can be fine-tuned. By enabling the creation of custom orchestrators on cheap hardware, PEARL democratizes the ability to build complex, autonomous AI systems, making them accessible to NGOs, universities, and small businesses.
2.6 RESEARCH GAPS
Based on the comprehensive review of the literature and trend analysis, several critical research gaps have been identified. These gaps represent the specific scientific and engineering problems that the PEARL framework is designed to solve.
2.6.1 THE OFFLINE FUNCTIONALITY GAP 
While individual components for offline AI exist (e.g., local STT with Whisper, local LLMs with Llama-2), there is a significant lack of integrated, end-to-end offline orchestration pipelines. Most existing "local" solutions are hybrid  they might run the LLM locally but still rely on cloud-based vector stores or external APIs for orchestration logic (e.g., using OpenAI functions for tool calling).
Literature shows no unified framework that simultaneously integrates offline retrieval, offline generation, and offline orchestration logic on a single low-power device. This "Offline Functionality Gap" prevents the deployment of sophisticated multi-agent systems in environments with strict air-gapping requirements, such as defense, healthcare, or rural education. PEARL addresses this by providing a blueprint for a fully self-contained stack where every component—from the controller to the database—runs on the edge.
2.6.2 THE ORCHESTRATION DISTILLATION GAP 
Knowledge Distillation has been extensively applied to semantic tasks (classification, translation) and reasoning tasks (Chain-of-Thought). However, there is a conspicuous absence of research on Orchestration Distillation. Current literature does not explore whether the managerial logic of a multi-agent system (i.e., the decision to route a task to Agent A vs. Agent B, or to loop back for verification) can be compressed into a small model.
Existing small models are typically fine-tuned to be "better answerers," not "better managers." There is no established methodology for training a small model to act as a Router or Coordinator based on expert traces. This gap means that even if we have efficient specialist agents, we lack an efficient "brain" to coordinate them.  2.7 LIMITATIONS
The current landscape of Multi-Agent Systems and Retrieval-Augmented Generation is defined by several critical, persistent, and often complementary limitations that make widespread implementation complex, prevents scalability, and hinders ethical deployment in sensitive sectors.
2.7.1 THE CLOUD-ORCHESTRATION PARADOX
One of the foundational problems is the Cloud-Orchestration Paradox. To achieve high-quality routing and planning, systems currently depend on state-of-the-art models (GPT-4, Claude 3) which are exclusively available via cloud APIs. This dependency inevitably leads to a loss of Data Sovereignty [23, 26]. Advanced RAG systems still frequently transmit user queries, retrieved documents, and internal logic traces to remote servers for processing. This server-side processing violates the strict confidentiality requirements of legal, medical, and defense sectors [27]. Researchers have highlighted that even if a system claims to be "private," the act of sending the orchestration prompts to a third party constitutes a breach of the air-gap, rendering the system unusable for classified or privileged workflows.
2.7.2 ECONOMIC UNVIABILITY OF MULTI-AGENT SYSTEMS
Deploying agentic systems at scale presents an immense financial barrier. Frameworks like MetaGPT or LangChain, used in many research prototypes, are prohibitively expensive to operate in production [11]. A single complex query might trigger 10-15 internal API calls (planning, retrieving, critiquing, generating), costing upwards of $0.50 per interaction. For public institutions like libraries or legal aid clinics, this recurring cost is unsustainable. Furthermore, many edge AI prototypes that attempt to solve this often run successfully only on costly dedicated GPU platforms like the NVIDIA Jetson Orin or A100 clusters [10], rather than consumer-grade hardware. This high operational cost and accessibility barrier are the greatest factors limiting widespread adoption in resource-constrained environments.
2.7.3 THE LATENCY BOTTLENECKS IN SEQUENTIAL REASONING
Both cutting-edge LLMs and sophisticated retrieval pipelines demand significant computational time. In cloud-based orchestration, the Round-Trip Latency—the time taken for data to travel to the server, be processed by a massive model, and return—often exceeds 2-3 seconds per step. In a multi-step agentic workflow, this accumulates to 10-15 seconds of wait time, which is unacceptable for real-time interaction [9]. While some attempts have been made to run LLMs on edge devices (like Raspberry Pi), they often report slow response latency (e.g., ~2.2 seconds per token) without heavy optimization [26]. The challenge lies in adapting enterprise-level reasoning (Chain-of-Thought) to the constrained resources of standard CPUs without destroying the user experience.
2.7.4 RIGIDITY VS INTELLIGENCE - TRADEOFF
Existing solutions suffer from a stark dichotomy  they are either Rigid but Fast or Intelligent but Slow. Rule-based systems (using if-then logic) are fast and cheap but fail fragility when faced with ambiguous user queries [11]. Conversely, Agentic systems (using LLMs) are flexible and intelligent but slow and expensive. Rarely do systems find a middle ground that integrates the robust handling of ambiguity with the speed of compiled code. This disparity creates a "fragmented ecosystem" where developers must choose between a dumb, reliable bot or a smart, unusable one. Navigating this trade-off presents a significant engineering hurdle, suggesting that the current model architectures are ill-suited for the holistic, responsive care required by professional users.
2.8 FUTURE DIRECTIONS
This analysis of past work points toward Integrated Edge Intelligence, indicating that future systems must move beyond cloud dependence, weaker performance, and single-task oriented processing. The next generation of orchestration frameworks would likely feature 
* Cross-Domain Generalization  Instead of training orchestrators for a single vertical (like Law), future research will focus on Generalizable Policy Networks. These systems will use transfer learning to apply routing logic learned in one domain (e.g., "Verify citations in legal texts") to analogous tasks in another (e.g., "Verify references in medical journals"), reducing the data requirements for training new specialized agents.
* Active Learning and Online Distillation  Systems must be capable of Self-Correction on the Edge. Future orchestrators will not be static; they will incorporate feedback loops where user corrections (e.g., "That's not what I asked, search deeper") are used to update the student model locally. 













3. DESIGN
3.1 INTRODUCTION TO SYSTEM DESIGN
The design of the PEARL (Performance-Efficient Agentic RAG through Learned Orchestration) framework is grounded in the critical necessity for a low-cost, reliable, privacy-preserving, and fully offline assistive platform tailored to the specific rigors of the legal domain. In stark contrast to contemporary orchestration systems that rely heavily on cloud-dependent Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4, the proposed PEARL architecture runs entirely on local hardware (standard CPU/GPU servers). It implements a novel knowledge distillation approach where orchestration capabilities—routing, planning, and verification—are transferred from a massive teacher model to a compact, quantized Flan-T5-small model.
This section provides a proposed design framework, a detailed explanation of components, operational flows, hardware integration, and software methodologies working in concert to enable PEARL to act as an intelligent, autonomous legal research assistant. The system utilizes a layered, modular architecture that allows each component (sensing, inference, scheduling, conversation, user interaction) to act independently while contributing to a collective goal  providing accurate, citation-backed legal information with zero data leakage.
Centered around key priorities, offline intelligence is paramount to ensure user privacy and compliance with data sovereignty laws. Low-latency processing is critical for providing timely research responses, replacing the multi-second lag of cloud APIs with millisecond-level local inference. High reliability is maintained via deterministic routing policies and comprehensive fallback mechanisms. Above all, cost-effectiveness is a major design goal; by eliminating per-token API costs, PEARL makes advanced legal AI accessible to under-resourced institutions like legal aid clinics and public libraries.


3.2 PROPOSED PROJECT FRAMEWORK
The proposed system framework for PEARL is a six-layer autonomous cognitive architecture. The collection of these layers represents the complete transformation pipeline from raw user input to intelligent, verified legal output. They collectively define how PEARL senses the user's intent, interprets complex legal queries, manages workflow states, interacts naturally, and stores contextual knowledge for continuous improvement.
The six layers are 
* Core Infrastructure Layer  The physical and virtual compute foundation.
* Query Processing & Analysis Layer  The input transformation and understanding engine.
* AI Orchestration & Intelligence Layer  The cognitive "brain" comprising the distilled model.
* Multi-Agent Execution Layer  The specialized workers performing retrieval and generation.
* Response Generation & Verification Layer  The output synthesis and fact-checking stage.
* Evaluation & Knowledge Management Layer  The long-term memory and quality assurance system.
This framework enables PEARL to behave as a fully autonomous orchestration system capable of operating offline with deterministic reliability.
3.2.1 HIGH-LEVEL SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
The high-level architecture visualizes the separation of concerns within the PEARL system. It demonstrates the flow of data from the User Interface down to the persistent Knowledge Base and back up through the Verification layers.

Figure 3.1  High-Level System Architecture of PEARL
Layer 1  Core Infrastructure Architecture
The first layer of this framework is the Core Infrastructure Layer, which serves as the physical and computational base required for intelligence, sensing, and user interaction. Unlike cloud-native systems that abstract away the hardware, PEARL is designed to run on specific local configurations to ensure performance.
PEARL operates on a standardized Python 3.10+ runtime environment, leveraging the PyTorch deep learning framework for model inference. The system is optimized for standard computing infrastructure, requiring approximately 8GB of RAM minimum (16GB recommended) to hold the vector database indices and model weights in memory. While optional, GPU acceleration (via CUDA) is supported to reduce inference latency from ~150ms to ~20ms.
For persistent knowledge storage, ChromaDB serves as the backbone. It stores high-dimensional vector embeddings of over 21,000 Indian legal documents, including statutes, Supreme Court judgments, and case law summaries. The infrastructure layer handles the intricate management of these embeddings, ensuring fast Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) search operations. Additionally, the Model Storage component manages the lifecycle of the Flan-T5-small weights (~300MB), Sentence-Transformer models, and tokenizer files, ensuring they are loaded efficiently into memory upon system boot. Configuration Management handles API keys (for optional cloud fallbacks), model paths, and agent settings through centralized YAML files, allowing for easy deployment across different environments without code changes.
Layer 2  Query Processing and Analysis Architecture
The transformation of raw, unstructured user queries into structured, machine-analyzable representations happens in the Query Processing and Analysis Layer. This layer acts as the gatekeeper, ensuring that only valid, well-formed data reaches the orchestration engine.
The Query Input Handler acts as the primary entry point, capable of receiving requests from multiple interfaces including a Streamlit Web UI, a FastAPI REST endpoint, or a Command Line Interface (CLI). It performs immediate input validation, sanitizing text to prevent injection attacks and normalizing formats. 
The core intelligence of this layer lies in the Query Complexity Analyzer. Before the main orchestrator is even invoked, this module performs a heuristic assessment of the query. It evaluates factors such as token length, the presence of specific legal entities (e.g., "Section 302", "Kesavananda Bharati"), and semantic density. It categorizes the query into complexity classes (Simple Fact, Complex Reasoning, Comparative Analysis). This pre-computation is critical for the orchestrator to make informed routing decisions with high reliability; for instance, a "Simple Fact" query might bypass the costly verification stage, whereas a "Comparative Analysis" will mandate it.
Layer 3  AI Orchestration & Cognitive Intelligence
This layer acts as the cognitive backbone of PEARL. It is responsible for the higher-order reasoning required to plan workflows. It comprises three major sub-system components  the Flan-T5 Orchestrator, the Query Analysis Engine, and the Workflow Optimizer.
The Flan-T5 Orchestrator utilizes a quantized, fine-tuned 80M-parameter Flan-T5-small model. Unlike standard classifiers, this model acts as a sequence-to-sequence generator. It takes the pre-processed query and complexity features as input and generates a structured "Workflow Token Sequence" as output. For example, for a complex legal query, it might predict the sequence 
 BOOST--> RETRIEVE--> GENERATE-->VERIFY. This prediction is based on thousands of expert traces distilled from GPT-4, allowing the small model to mimic the planning capabilities of a much larger teacher.

Figure  3.2  Orchestration Decision Flow
The Workflow Optimizer is a deterministic logic layer that sits downstream from the AI model. It post-processes the predicted sequence to ensure logical validity. It enforces dependency rules (e.g., "An Answering Agent cannot run before a Retriever Agent") and performs redundancy checks. If the Orchestrator predicts a retrieval step but the confidence score for the query's clarity is low, the Optimizer dynamically inserts a "Prompt Booster" step to rephrase the query first. This hybrid approach of AI prediction + Rule-based Optimization ensures that workflows are both intelligent and robust.
Layer 4  Multi-Agent Execution Architecture
All agent coordination and task execution are governed by this layer using an event-driven architecture. Each step in the optimized workflow triggers a specific microservice or function.
The Agent Coordinator manages the lifecycle of five specialized agents 
* Prompt Booster Agent 
 This agent utilizes a few-shot prompting strategy with a local SLM to expand vague user queries. For example, it transforms "divorce rules" into "legal grounds for divorce under Hindu Marriage Act 1955 and Special Marriage Act".
* Retriever Agent  
This agent interfaces with the ChromaDB vector store. It performs semantic similarity searches using embeddings generated by sentence-transformers. It retrieves the top-k document chunks and extracts metadata such as Case ID, Court Name, and Judgment Date.
* Answering Agent  
This agent is responsible for synthesizing the final response. It takes the retrieved context and the user query to generate a natural language answer. It strictly adheres to a "Citation-First" policy, ensuring every claim is backed by a source document.


* Citation Verifier Agent  
A unique component of PEARL, this agent performs a post-hoc check. It calculates the semantic overlap between the generated sentences and the retrieved source text. If the overlap is below a threshold (e.g., 0.7), it flags the statement as potentially hallucinated.

Figure 3.3   Multi-Agent Execution Flow


Layer 5  Response Generation & Verification
This serves as PEARL’s expressive interface. The Response Formatter structures the raw text into a user-friendly format. It organizes the output into sections  "Summary," "Detailed Legal Analysis," "Relevant Case Laws," and "Statutory References."
Crucially, the Confidence Calculator aggregates scores from multiple stages  the Orchestrator's routing confidence, the Retriever's similarity scores, and the Verifier's overlap scores. It presents a unified "Reliability Score" to the user. If the score is low, the system transparently warns the user that the information may need further verification. This layer ensures that the output is not just text, but actionable, trustworthy legal intelligence.
Layer 6  Evaluation & Knowledge Management
This layer handles all persistent data generated by PEARL. The Data Storage subsystem manages the ChromaDB instances and the SQLite logs for interaction history. Unlike transient systems, PEARL remembers.
The Evaluation Engine runs in the background, calculating metrics like RAS (Routing Accuracy Score) and WAI (Workflow Appropriateness Index) for every interaction. These metrics compare the student model's decisions against a set of heuristic baselines or recorded expert traces. This continuous evaluation loop allows developers to monitor the system's "IQ" over time and identify drift. The training data repository accumulates successful interaction traces, which can be used to further fine-tune the Flan-T5 orchestrator in future updates (Online Learning).
3.3 SYSTEM BEHAVIOR
The proposed framework of PEARL follows an event-driven behavioural model. When the system boots, the core infrastructure initializes  the Flan-T5 model is loaded into VRAM, the ChromaDB connection is established, and the API endpoints go live.
When a user submits a query, PEARL enters Orchestration Mode. The input is frozen, analyzed for complexity, and passed to the Flan-T5 model. The model predicts a graph of agents. The system then transitions to Execution Mode, where the Agent Coordinator steps through this graph. It manages state passing—ensuring the output of the Retriever becomes the input of the Answerer.
Finally, the system enters Verification & Response Mode. The generated text is scrutinized by the Verifier agent. Only after passing validity checks is the response streamed to the user. Simultaneously, a Logging Event is triggered, archiving the query, the chosen path, and the result for future analysis. This behavioural pipeline ensures that the user is provided with safety, memory preservation, and high-quality assistance while ensuring the system remains robust against errors.













4. CONCLUSION
This work presented the design and architectural blueprint of PEARL (Performance-Efficient Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation through Learned Orchestration), a privacy-preserving and offline-capable agentic RAG framework intended for deployment in resource-constrained and regulation-sensitive domains such as legal research. The primary focus of this project was to conceptualize and formally define an alternative to cloud-dependent orchestration systems by transferring orchestration intelligence from large language models into compact, locally deployable models through knowledge distillation.
Through an extensive literature survey and critical review, this study identified key limitations in existing multi-agent RAG systems, including high operational cost, cloud dependency, latency bottlenecks, and lack of orchestration-specific evaluation metrics. In response, the proposed PEARL framework introduces a learned orchestration paradigm, where workflow routing and agent coordination are treated as a prediction problem rather than a rule-based or cloud-driven process. The design emphasizes modularity, offline execution, deterministic workflow validation, and compliance-by-design principles, ensuring data sovereignty and auditability.
The system architecture was developed in detail, outlining the roles of the orchestration controller, multi-agent execution layers, retrieval and verification mechanisms, and evaluation components.It is important to note that the framework, workflows, and metrics described in this report are conceptual and design-oriented, and no empirical implementation or experimental validation has been conducted as part of the present work.
Overall, this project establishes a strong theoretical and architectural foundation for building efficient, offline, and cost-effective agentic RAG systems. The proposed design demonstrates the feasibility of decoupling orchestration intelligence from large cloud-based models and repositioning it within compact, locally deployable controllers. Implementation, fine-tuning, quantitative evaluation, and real-world deployment of the PEARL framework are identified as future work, and this report serves as a structured roadmap to guide those subsequent phases.
